Developers often construct modular monoliths and microservices with clean structures and distinct projects, yet frequently encounter changes rippling unexpectedly through their systems. This phenomenon, exemplified by an HR module update affecting authentication or a vehicle compliance change impacting shipment planning, stems from a fundamental misunderstanding: defining boundaries solely based on physical aspects or data, rather than logical ownership. The core challenge is conflating physical boundaries—such as processes, databases, queues, or deployable units—with the underlying logical boundaries that define a system's true modularity. When modules appear to be split but retain deep coupling, it indicates a failure to establish clear ownership over distinct concepts.

The speaker emphasizes that logical boundaries are about encapsulating ownership over behavior, business rules, lifecycle, meaning, and authority, independent of physical deployment. A single real-world entity, like a 'vehicle' or 'employee', often represents multiple distinct logical concepts across different system domains (e.g., a 'vehicle' for compliance vs. for dispatch; an 'employee' in HR vs. a 'user' in IT). Sharing a singular conceptual model, often termed an 'entity service', inevitably leads to tight coupling, where a change driven by one module's 'reason to change' inadvertently affects others. While physical boundaries are crucial for enforcing isolation, they do not inherently define the correct logical separation. True modularity dictates that each logical boundary maintains its internal model, exposing only necessary integrations. This principle extends to physical infrastructure, where logical boundaries can span multiple repositories, frontend/backend components, or even share a single database instance with schema-level ownership. Effective boundary definition, therefore, begins with identifying who truly owns a concept, its data, and its associated behaviors, ensuring independent evolution and minimizing system-wide ripple effects.
