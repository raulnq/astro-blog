A recent Bloomberg report has cast doubt on the capabilities and immediate availability of Salesforce's AI platform, Agent Force, echoing prior social media criticisms labeling it 'vaporware.' The report suggests that core functionalities, such as AI agents designed to interact with clients and patients to provide practical knowledge, remain unavailable. Specifically, Bloomberg indicates that the advertised chatbot for Agent Force is still in testing and not widely accessible to most web visitors, a claim corroborated by articles like Gizmodo's 'Salesforce has an AI vaporware problem.' This scrutiny arises amidst a broader industry debate concerning the 'SaaS apocalypse,' a theory positing that AI will enable such facile application development that Software-as-a-Service models will become obsolete. However, a veteran web developer with three decades of experience, having successfully launched several small, profitable SaaS products, dismisses this 'SaaS apocalypse' as unfounded hype reminiscent of the dot-com bubble.

The seasoned developer characterizes AI as a powerful utility rather than a job-destroying entity or a useless gimmick. Drawing from extensive personal experience and discussions with both non-coders and seasoned developers, the expert notes that while AI can quickly enable non-developers to achieve an '80% wall' – a semi-functional state – they often struggle to reach production readiness due to a lack of structured developer thinking. Conversely, for professional developers, AI acts as a 'superpower,' potentially yielding a '5 to 10x improvement' in coding productivity for those who understand system design and how to effectively 'work with the AI properly.' While useful for rapid prototyping and building small, one-off utility applications, caution is advised regarding 'agentic development,' where AI operates autonomously; such outputs, much like a junior developer's work, require thorough review and double-checking. The expert predicts a '25 to 35% overall increase in productivity' in the foreseeable future, emphasizing AI's potential to automate 'boring parts' of software development. Learning the AI stack and foundational development principles (design patterns, refactoring, web stack) is crucial, as the challenges of bringing software to market extend far beyond mere coding. Furthermore, claims of widespread job displacement due to AI are largely overstated, with layoffs primarily attributed to post-COVID overhiring and shifting market demands, rather than AI-driven automation.
