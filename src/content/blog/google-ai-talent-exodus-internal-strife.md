Google's AI division is experiencing significant churn, marked by the departure of several prominent researchers to competitors. After 14 years with Google, including nearly nine at DeepMind, a key individual has joined Anthropic. This move follows the earlier departures of top AI researchers Jonas Adler and Alexander Pritzell to Anthropic, and Gnome, acquired from Character AI for $2.7 billion, to OpenAI. These high-profile exits underscore a growing talent drain, occurring as Google DeepMind acknowledges falling behind in critical areas of AI development. Internally, a 'strike team' involving Sergey Brin has been formed to bolster coding models, reflecting a realization that Google lags behind Anthropic and OpenAI in agents and coding capabilities, despite possessing the world's largest internal codebase. Further exacerbating these concerns, the launch of Gemini 3.5 Pro has been delayed from June to July, with DeepMind reportedly 'not satisfied' with the model's current state.

While Gemini models are recognized for their robust knowledge integration and spatial reasoning, consistently topping specialized benchmarks, their practical application is hindered by behavioral deficiencies. These models frequently exhibit issues with long-horizon tasks and coherent tool utilization, often entering reasoning loops or misinterpreting instructions. This performance gap is attributed to DeepMind's foundational focus as a heavy research group, prioritizing the scientific advancement of model intelligence over the development of pipelines for training behavioral data through reinforcement learning from human-LLM interactions—a methodology effectively leveraged by agile competitors like Cursor. Google's reliance on its vast internal codebase for training data is also questioned, with insights suggesting that code histories, not just code volume, are crucial for effective coding models. Evidence of this strategic gap includes Google's use of its Anti-gravity harness to gather behavioral data from users interacting with Anthropic's Opus 4.5 model, indicating a need for external data sources to compensate for internal shortcomings.

The internal culture at Google is identified as a significant impediment to innovation, exemplified by the termination of Justin, the creator of the viral Google Workspace CLI. Despite widespread positive reception, hitting number one on Hacker News, and rapid adoption, Justin was reportedly fired amidst concerns from Workspace leadership about potential disruption and legal scrutiny over branding. This contrasts sharply with the environments at Anthropic and OpenAI, where similar 'hack projects' like Claude Code and OpenAI's Codex, respectively, were fostered and scaled into foundational product successes. The incident highlights a 'fundamental failure' within Google's corporate structure that may disincentivize grassroots innovation. Critics suggest Google, despite its unparalleled resources in compute, data, and capital, is struggling to translate these advantages into a competitive lead in frontier AI, partly due to an internal resistance to embracing disruptive, bottom-up initiatives.
