Justin Pernald, a Google engineer, was reportedly fired two months ago following the creation of a Google Workspace Command Line Interface (CLI). Despite its placement within an official Google GitHub organization, the tool was explicitly labeled as 'not an officially supported Google product.' The Workspace CLI, designed to enable interaction with services like Drive, Gmail, and Calendar via the command line, quickly gained significant traction, reaching number one on Hacker News and garnering thousands of GitHub stars and users within days. This popularity is attributed to the rising proficiency of AI agents in utilizing well-documented command-line tools, a trend exemplified by Peter Steinberger's unofficial GOG CLI for his OpenClaw agent platform.

The firing has ignited discussions within the developer community, questioning Google's rationale behind terminating an engineer for a widely adopted tool that offered strategic benefits in the burgeoning AI agent space. While Google's internal processes for releasing public repositories often require legal approval, Pernald’s DevRel team routinely created open-source abstractions, suggesting a potential clash with established protocols or a shift in enforcement. Eddy Osmani, another long-tenured Googler, also recently departed, having promoted the same unofficial CLI, raising speculation about a coordinated internal pushback. This incident is seen by some as symptomatic of internal politics and inconsistent product strategies, where different teams might prioritize human interaction with applications over CLI-driven agent usage, potentially hindering Google’s progress in agentic coding where competitors like OpenAI and Anthropic are perceived to have an edge. The situation underscores the challenges Google faces in navigating rapid AI advancements, fostering internal innovation, and retaining top talent in a highly competitive landscape.
