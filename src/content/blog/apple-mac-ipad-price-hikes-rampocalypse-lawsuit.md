Apple has officially rolled out substantial price increases across its Mac, iPad, and select home product lines, with average hikes reportedly hitting 20% and some products seeing surges up to 36%. Notable adjustments include the MacBook Neo by €100 (14%), the MacBook Air 13-inch by €230 (19%), and the MacBook Pro M5 Max by a significant €600. Desktop Macs also reflect this trend, with the iMac experiencing a €300 increase and Mac Studio models seeing price jumps of €700 for the M4 Max and €1500 for the M3 Ultra. While the Mac Mini M4's price point remains stable, its base configuration has been quietly downgraded from 512GB to 256GB storage, effectively increasing the cost for equivalent capacity by an additional €120. iPad models are also more expensive, ranging from €120 to €200 extra, with Apple TV and HomePod products also affected. The primary driver cited for these widespread price adjustments is the escalating cost of RAM and SSDs, which have reportedly surged by up to 100% in recent months.

This market volatility for memory components is projected to persist until at least 2028, with some forecasts extending to 2030. The situation is further complicated by a federal lawsuit filed in California, alleging that major chip manufacturers Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron have engaged in coordinated efforts to artificially reduce memory supply and inflate prices by as much as 700%. This complaint, dubbed "Rampocalypse," draws parallels to a 2000 DRAM price-fixing scandal where these same companies were found guilty. In response to the soaring costs and alleged cartel behavior, Apple is reportedly lobbying the U.S. government to consider procuring memory from CXMT, China's largest RAM manufacturer, despite the company being on the Pentagon's blacklist. Critics highlight the market's oligopolistic structure, where a limited number of players may lack sufficient competition, leading to potentially exploitative pricing practices. The tech community is closely monitoring judicial outcomes and the broader implications for electronic product viability, anticipating significant shifts in global memory sourcing.
