Following a unanimous jury verdict in Epic Games' favor, Google has announced significant reforms to its Play Store policies, globally resolving a protracted antitrust dispute. Dubbed 'A new era for choice and openness,' these updates aim to enhance developer and user flexibility. Key changes include expanded billing options, allowing developers to use their own in-app payment systems or direct users to external websites. Google is also introducing a program for 'registered app stores' to streamline sideloading, though the specifics of 'quality and safety benchmarks' for participation remain a point of interest for the developer community. Furthermore, Google Play has revised its fee structure: in-app purchase service fees will decrease to 20% (15% for new installs), with an additional 5% fee if developers opt to use Google Play's billing system. Recurring subscriptions will see a 10% fee. Epic Games' Tim Sweeney confirmed the global settlement of their dispute with Google following these announcements.

Despite these concessions, a parallel initiative called 'Keep Android Open' raises concerns about future restrictions on the platform. Google plans to mandate central developer registration for all Android apps by September 2026, requiring a fee, government identification, linking of signing keys, and listing all application identifiers. While Google asserts this measure is crucial for combating rising malware and scamware, particularly with AI-generated threats, critics argue it fundamentally shifts Android's open-source ethos. This change could impede anonymous developers, such as those creating emulators, and prevent direct sharing of unsigned applications without Google's explicit approval. Concurrently, the EU has mandated Google allow competitors access to Android features for AI services, further fragmenting the platform's control points. These developments highlight a continuing tension between platform security, developer freedom, and market competition within the Android ecosystem.
