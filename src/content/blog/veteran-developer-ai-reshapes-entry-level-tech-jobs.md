A veteran software developer with three decades of industry experience posits that the perceived disappearance of entry-level tech jobs is a recurring cycle in the industry, not an AI-driven apocalypse. Analogizing to past paradigm shifts like the decline of ActionScript, VB6, Pearl CGI, and traditional Java stacks, the expert states that core entry-level roles—such as those focused solely on raw React or basic HTML/JavaScript—are indeed diminishing. However, this is presented as a normal evolution, not a net loss, for those who adapt. The critical shift, according to this perspective, is the integration of AI proficiency with foundational skills across all sectors. This new paradigm necessitates a pragmatic understanding of AI's current limitations, including its propensity for "hallucinations," flawed assumptions, and lack of true logical capacity, as evidenced by a recent Apple study where leading AI models "failed miserably" on new, mirrored benchmark tests designed to bypass training biases.

The expert advocates for viewing AI as a "world's fastest total junior" – a powerful associative array requiring human oversight and verification due to its inherent inability to "think" or produce truly novel insights. While AI is expected to deliver a significant 25-35% productivity gain, accelerating software development cycles, this is projected to create a "huge boom" in new jobs and use cases, echoing the internet's transformative impact on industries like publishing. The new entry-level developer, therefore, must master foundational web technologies (HTML, CSS, JavaScript, request/response cycles) coupled with advanced AI skills, including understanding different models, orchestration, prompt engineering, and integration into business workflows. This dispels the "spec writer" myth, emphasizing an iterative, component-based development approach where AI acts as a collaborative tool for generating boilerplate code under expert direction, rather than autonomously building monolithic applications. The current layoffs are attributed to a market readjustment, favoring those with AI competencies and disfavoring those resistant to upskilling.
