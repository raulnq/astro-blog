A prominent case involving domain registrar Namecheap and founder Sniga Sir is sending ripples through the startup community, underscoring the precarious nature of digital assets. Sniga Sir, founder of The Juggernaut, a startup dedicated to South Asian stories, saw her company's website (thejuggernaut.com) globally shut down by Namecheap in January 2025. After securing a temporary restraining order (TRO) from an American court that ruled in her favor, Namecheap subsequently filed a personal counter-suit against her.

The initial shutdown, which crippled The Juggernaut's growth, usage, and investor conversations, reportedly stemmed from an Indian trademark dispute concerning the generic term "Juggernaut." Despite the American court ruling that Sniga Sir could retain her domain, provided it was geofenced for India, Namecheap not only defied this but also sought exorbitant legal fees. Namecheap’s terms of service claim broad discretion to terminate domains "at any time for any reason." Further escalating tensions, Namecheap's former CEO publicly accused Sir of "public lies and manipulation" and issued veiled threats, while the registrar's legal counsel threatened Sir and her firm with "civil liability and ethical consequences" should they publicly discuss the case. Industry commentators highlight the incident as a stark example of how legal processes can be weaponized to exhaust smaller entities, drawing parallels to historical tactics used by large organizations to overwhelm opponents with litigation rather than merit.

This dispute has spurred a broader discussion among tech professionals about the inherent risks associated with domain ownership and the selection of registrars. Experts advise founders to opt for registrars whose primary revenue streams are not solely dependent on domain fees and who demonstrate a strong sensitivity to public reputation. Companies that openly admit errors and actively work to resolve customer issues are deemed more reliable. The incident serves as a critical cautionary tale, urging founders to meticulously vet their domain providers and understand the complex layers of registry, registrar, and TLD governance, all of which can impact a business's operational continuity and legal standing.
